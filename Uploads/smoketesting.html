Smoke Test Report

Why is Smoke Testing Necessary

Smoke testing is one of the most cost-effective methods your utility can use to locate breaks and defects in the public sewer system (sewer mains, service laterals, cross or illegal connections). The smoke is an indication of inflow and infiltration (I&I) that can enter a sewer system during a rain event. This reduces the capacity at the sewer plant, increases treatment costs and can lead to overburdening of the sanitary system, possibly leading to overflows and backups.

How Smoke Testing Works

Blowers are placed in adjoining manholes and a non-toxic liquid smoke is forced through the blowers into the sanitary or storm sewer. Defects in the pipe or manhole structures will create a pathway for the smoke to escape thereby identifying the location of the defects. While smoke is being forced into the sewer, field crews search adjacent areas looking for smoke. When smoke is found, all characteristics are documented including photos and GPS point.

Definitions

Defect Number

Defects are listed sequentially beginning with the number 1, followed by 2, 3, 4, and so on.

Defect Type

This is the type of defect. Common types of defects are broken cleanouts, or cleanout cap missing.

Inflow Potential

A numerical score from 1 to 5. 5 indicates inflow could be severe. A score of 1 indicates little to no inflow is possible. This value is the primary indicator to rank the defect.

Smoke Intensity

A 1-5 score of the intensity of the smoke, with 5 being most severe and 1 being least severe.

Address

The closest address to the location of smoke.

Test Number

One smoke test can generate up to dozens of smoke locations.

Follow Up Actions

Typically during a smoke test, many defects are located. These defects are ranked in terms of severity and are shown on following tables and maps. Follow up CCTV for major defects is required on mainline defects in order to determine corrective measures.
